    \section{Introduction and Data Presentation}

    In this project, we utilize the \textit{Sleep Health and Lifestyle} dataset, which records health and lifestyle information for a sample of individuals. The variables in this dataset can be classified into three distinct categories:

    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Demographic and Professional Data: Unique Identifier, Age, Gender, and Occupation.
        \item Lifestyle Indicators: Physical Activity Level, Daily Steps, Sleep Duration, and Sleep Quality (subjective scale).
        \item Physiological Biomarkers: Body Mass Index (BMI), Heart Rate, Blood Pressure (in systolic/diastolic format), and Stress Level.
    \end{enumerate}

    The dataset also contains a categorical target variable, Sleep Disorder (indicating the presence of insomnia, sleep apnea, or the absence of a disorder).

    
    
    \section{Problem Statement and Objectives}

    In the medical field, early detection of sleep disorders is crucial for preventing serious cardiovascular complications. The challenge is not only to predict correctly but, more importantly, not to miss pathological cases. \newline

    Our problem statement is as follows: \newline
    \textbf{How can we maximize the detection of sleep disorders (Insomnia and Apnea) using lifestyle data and simple biomarkers, while minimizing the risk of false negatives?} \newline

    Our objectives are:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Identify the most influential factors on sleep health.
        \item Compare different supervised learning models (Logistic Regression, SVM, Random Forest, XGBoost).
        \item Optimize the selected model to maximize Recall, a critical metric in medical diagnosis.
        \item Evaluate the possibility of reducing the number of necessary variables without performance loss.
    \end{itemize}

    \section{Methodological Approach}

    \subsection{Data Preprocessing}
    We first cleaned and transformed the raw data:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Categorical Variable Encoding}:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textit{Occupation}: This variable is nominal (no logical order between "Engineer" and "Lawyer"). We used One-Hot Encoding to prevent the model from incorrectly interpreting a numerical hierarchy.
            \item \textit{BMI Category} and \textit{Sleep Disorder}: These variables present an intrinsic progression of severity (e.g., Normal < Overweight < Obese). We opted for \textbf{Ordinal Encoding} to preserve this valuable ordinal information for prediction.
        \end{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Blood Pressure Processing}:
        The initial variable was provided as a "Systolic/Diastolic" string (e.g., "120/80"), which is mathematically unusable.
        \begin{itemize}
            \item We split it into two distinct numerical variables: \textit{Systolic BP} and \textit{Diastolic BP}.
            \item We then created a new synthetic variable, Pulse Pressure (Systolic - Diastolic). In cardiology, high pulse pressure is an indicator of arterial stiffness and a cardiovascular risk marker often correlated with sleep-disordered breathing.
        \end{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Normalization (StandardScaler)}:
        The dataset variables have very heterogeneous magnitudes (e.g., \textit{Daily Steps} $\approx$ 5000 vs \textit{Sleep Duration} $\approx$ 7).
        Since some algorithms (like SVM or KNN) are based on Euclidean distance calculations, large-scale variables would artificially dominate the decision. We therefore standardized all numerical variables to ensure an equitable contribution from each factor.
    \end{itemize}

    \subsection{Evaluation Protocol and Metrics}
    To ensure the robustness of our results against class imbalance, we adopted a Stratified Cross-Validation strategy with $k=5$. This method ensures that the proportion of each class (Normal, Insomnia, Apnea) is preserved in each training and test fold.

    The preferred performance metric is  recall. In a medical diagnostic context, the absolute priority is to minimize False Negatives, i.e., to avoid classifying a patient suffering from a pathology as "healthy". We also examine Confusion Matrices to finely analyze classification errors between different pathologies.

    \subsection{Candidate Models}
    We selected a diverse panel of algorithms to cover different learning approaches:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Logistic Regression}: For its simplicity and interpretability (linear model).
        \item \textbf{Support Vector Machine (SVM)}: For its ability to find complex separating hyperplanes in high-dimensional spaces.
        \item \textbf{Random Forest}: An ensemble method robust to overfitting and capable of capturing non-linearities.
        \item \textbf{XGBoost}: A Gradient Boosting method recognized for its high performance on structured tabular data.
    \end{itemize}

    \subsection{Experimental Strategy}
    Our selection process took place in three iterative phases, guided by a desire for simplification and clinical applicability:

    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Phase 1: Baseline Evaluation} \\
        Initially, we trained all models on all available variables. The objective was to establish a theoretical maximum performance level without variable selection constraints.

        \item \textbf{Phase 2: Noise Elimination (Exclusion of Occupation)} \\
        Our preliminary analyses suggesting that profession (\textit{Occupation}) did not have a strong causal link with sleep disorders, we tested the models without these variables. The hypothesis was that these features could introduce noise or Overfitting without bringing real predictive gain.

        \item \textbf{Phase 3: "Clinical Biomarkers" Approach (Minimalist)} \\
        From a perspective of real-world deployment and diagnostic cost reduction, we sought to determine if a high-performing model could be built solely from 5 key biomarkers: \textit{Age, Sleep Duration, Systolic BP, Diastolic BP, and BMI Category}. This approach aims to propose a lightweight pre-diagnostic tool, not requiring a complex lifestyle questionnaire.
    \end{enumerate}

    \subsection{Comparative Analysis and Model Choice}
    Comparing the three phases reveals a remarkable result: moving from a full dataset to only 5 biomarkers results in negligible performance loss, or even none for some models.

    The \textbf{XGBoost} model, in particular, maintains a Recall of \textbf{0.8827} with the biomarkers, which is a score almost identical to its performance with all variables (0.8811) and very close to the best model from Phase 1 (Random Forest: 0.8862).

    This robustness of XGBoost to variable reduction, combined with its execution speed, motivates our choice to retain it as the final model. The "Minimalist Biomarkers" approach is therefore validated: it offers equivalent diagnostic accuracy while being much simpler to deploy in practice.

    \subsection{Hyperparameter Optimization}
    To further maximize the model's sensitivity (Recall), we proceeded with an exhaustive search for the best hyperparameters (Grid Search) for XGBoost. We explored different combinations of \textit{learning\_rate}, \textit{max\_depth}, \textit{n\_estimators}, and \textit{subsample}.

    The best identified configuration is as follows:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textit{learning\_rate} : 0.05
        \item \textit{max\_depth} : 4
        \item \textit{n\_estimators} : 100
        \item \textit{subsample} : 1.0
    \end{itemize}

    This optimization improved the cross-validation score, bringing the average Macro Recall to \textbf{0.8967}. This confirms the value of fine-tuning the model to capture the subtleties of decision boundaries between classes.

    \subsection{Exploration of Ensemble Methods}
    In an effort to push model performance to the maximum, we explored Ensemble Learning techniques. The idea was to combine the predictions of our four base models (Logistic Regression, SVM, Random Forest, XGBoost) to hope to reduce variance and improve generalization.

    We implemented three voting strategies:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Majority Vote (Hard Voting)}: The majority class among predictions wins.
        \item \textbf{Probability Averaging (Soft Voting)}: Average of probabilities predicted by each model.
        \item \textbf{Weighted Voting}: Weighted vote granting more importance to the best-performing models (Random Forest and XGBoost).
    \end{itemize}

    However, the results obtained were not conclusive. The best score obtained (via Soft Voting) caps at a Recall of 0.8783, which remains lower than the performance of our optimized XGBoost model alone (0.8967). The added complexity inherent in maintaining four models in parallel not being justified by a performance gain, we confirmed our choice to keep the single XGBoost model for the final solution.

    \subsection{Final Validation}
    To evaluate the real generalization capacity of our solution, we applied the optimized model to the validation dataset (a sample never seen during training or optimization).

    The final results are excellent:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Accuracy} : 0.9474
        \item \textbf{Recall (Macro)} : 0.9697
        \item \textbf{Precision (Macro)} : 0.9333
        \item \textbf{F1 Score (Macro)} : 0.9471
    \end{itemize}

The 4 cases of insomnia and 4 cases of apnea were correctly detected (0 False Negatives). The only error committed is a False Positive (a healthy individual classified as suffering from apnea), which is acceptable in a screening context where a false alarm is preferred to a missed diagnosis.

    \section{Conclusion}
    This project demonstrated that it is possible to predict sleep disorders (Insomnia and Apnea) with high reliability using a very limited number of physiological biomarkers (Age, BMI, Blood Pressure, Sleep Duration).

    Our approach, based on an optimized XGBoost model, achieves a Recall of nearly 97\% on validation data, perfectly fulfilling the objective of minimizing false negatives. The simplicity of the required data (5 variables) makes this model easily integratable into connected health tools or rapid pre-diagnostic protocols, thus offering a concrete solution for improving sleep disorder screening.
